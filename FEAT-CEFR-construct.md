## 1. Purpose

FEAT CEFR measures whether a candidate can communicate effectively at a specific CEFR level. It is designed as an alternative to traditional CEFR-aligned tests (Cambridge suite, IELTS, TOEFL, Pearson PTE) that emphasises performance in realistic scenarios over receptive knowledge and exam technique.

Target users include:
- Language learners seeking formal proficiency certification
- Job applicants needing CEFR evidence for employers
- Students proving language capability for academic entry
- Workers needing language credentials for visa, immigration, or professional contexts
- Organisations certifying staff language capability

Outcomes are used for language proficiency certification, expressed as a CEFR level (A1–C2) with a **Can / Not Yet** judgement at the level attempted.

The initial product focus is written performance. Spoken performance is planned as a parallel modality and will share the same construct, dimensions, and scoring logic.

---

## 2. Core Construct

**This assessment measures the ability to communicate effectively in real-world contexts at a given CEFR level, through written performance.**

**Successful performance looks like:** the candidate completes an authentic communicative task at the target CEFR level — the reader understands the message, can act on it if required, and recognises the language as appropriate to the context and level.

**Failure (Not Yet) looks like:** the communication fails. The reader cannot act on the message, key meaning is lost, or the language produced is demonstrably below the level attempted.

---

## 2a. Construct Boundaries

FEAT CEFR deliberately does **not** measure:

- **Academic reasoning.** Use FEAT Academic where the construct is thinking, not language. At C1/C2 the scenarios may look academic, but the judgement is about language use, not argument quality.
- **Workplace judgement.** Use FEAT Professional where the construct is decision-making. At C1/C2 the scenarios may look professional, but the judgement remains about communication.
- **Metalinguistic knowledge.** Candidates are not tested on grammar rules, linguistic terminology, or explicit knowledge of English structure.
- **Pronunciation or accent.** Out of scope for the written modality. Addressed in spoken FEAT CEFR when that launches.
- **Reading or listening comprehension as isolated skills.** The construct is productive communication.
- **Translation ability.** Candidates respond in English; the construct is not English-to-L1 fluency.

Boundaries exist to prevent misuse: a candidate scoring Can C1 on FEAT CEFR has demonstrated C1 written communication, not that they will succeed in a C1 workplace or academic environment where other constructs (judgement, reasoning) are also required.

---

## 3. Dimensions of Performance

| Dimension | What it represents | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Task Achievement** | Does the response accomplish the communicative task? | CEFR is functional — if the reader cannot act on it, the level has not been reached. |
| **Content Quality** | Is the substance appropriate to the context, audience, and level? | Communication requires appropriate content, not just surface language. |
| **Language Control** | Is grammar, vocabulary, and structural range sufficient for the level attempted? | CEFR levels are defined in part by control over language features. This dimension prevents Task Achievement from inflating scores on its own. |
| **Coherence** | Do ideas connect in a way the reader can follow? | Extended communication at higher levels requires paragraph- and discourse-level coherence, not just sentence-level accuracy. |

---

## 3a. The Product Twist

**CEFR = language as function, not form.** Where traditional CEFR-aligned tests reward grammatical perfection and discrete-item vocabulary recall, FEAT CEFR asks: did you communicate? Language control still matters — but it serves communication, not the other way round.

---

## 4. Performance Levels (CEFR bands, binary judgement)

**FEAT CEFR uses the six-level CEFR scale (A1 → C2), not the standard FEAT 4-tier scale.** Judgement is **binary — Can / Not Yet — at the level attempted.**

- A candidate attempts a task calibrated to a specific CEFR level.
- If the response meets that level's descriptors across all four dimensions → **Can [Level]**
- If it does not → **Not Yet [Level]**, with a secondary indicator of the level the response actually matches (e.g. *"Not Yet B2 — response sits at B1 for Language Control"*)
- A candidate's overall CEFR level is the highest level at which they achieve Can across the tasks attempted.

**Level descriptors (short form):**

- **A1** — Can communicate basic, predictable needs in routine, familiar contexts.
- **A2** — Can handle straightforward social and practical exchanges on familiar topics.
- **B1** — Can deal with most everyday situations, express opinions, and produce simple connected text.
- **B2** — Can communicate fluently on complex topics, argue a position, and interact without strain.
- **C1** — Can use language flexibly and effectively for social, academic, and professional purposes.
- **C2** — Can communicate with near-native precision, nuance, and fluency across any context.

## 5. Task Design Principles

**Every task must:**
- Be self-contained — the candidate has all the information needed to respond
- Present an authentic communicative scenario (email, message, post, note, report, article, analysis)
- Specify context, audience, and purpose
- Be calibrated to a specific CEFR level
- Reward communication that works, not grammatical perfection in isolation

**Every task must avoid:**
- Discrete-item grammar testing — that tests form, not communication
- Vocabulary recall without communicative context
- Translation tasks
- Cultural specificity that advantages candidates from particular backgrounds
- Reading-comprehension tasks disguised as writing tasks

---

## 6. Evidence Model

The candidate's written response is the sole source of evidence. From it we capture:

- Whether the communicative task is accomplished (Task Achievement signal)
- Whether content is appropriate for the context, audience, and level (Content Quality signal)
- Whether language range and accuracy match the target level's expectations (Language Control signal)
- Whether ideas connect across sentences and paragraphs (Coherence signal)

**We explicitly ignore:**
- Errors that do not impede communication (minor slips, spelling that doesn't obscure meaning)
- Handwriting, typing speed, formatting choices
- Candidate's first language or linguistic background
- Cultural style preferences that do not affect communication

**Minimum Evidence Requirement**

A response must be long enough and substantive enough to evaluate all four dimensions at the target level. The result is **Insufficient Evidence** rather than Not Yet when:

- The response is too short to demonstrate level features (e.g. a single sentence where a B2 task calls for extended writing)
- The response is written in a language other than English
- The response is off-topic or ignores the communicative purpose
- The response is copy-paste or a memorised template with no engagement

---

## 7. Scoring Model

Binary (Can / Not Yet) at the target CEFR level, informed by analytic judgements on the four dimensions.

- **All four dimensions must meet the level's descriptors** for a Can judgement at that level.
- If one or more dimensions fall short, the response receives **Not Yet** at the target level. A secondary indicator records the highest level actually achieved (e.g. *"Not Yet B2 — Language Control at B1"*), which is useful for placement and progression decisions.
- Overall CEFR level = the highest level at which the candidate achieves Can across the tasks attempted.

**Handling conflicting dimension scores**

- **Strong Task Achievement, weak Language Control** → a communicative learner who gets the message across with errors that place them below the target level. Scored Not Yet at target with level indicator. Common pattern; the candidate may well achieve Can at the level below.
- **Strong Language Control, weak Task Achievement** → the candidate writes well but missed the point. Scored Not Yet — language without communication does not reach the level, even if the sentences are elegant.
- **Uneven profiles** (large gaps between dimensions) are flagged for reviewer attention. CEFR is holistic, and wide dimension spreads usually indicate inconsistent candidate capability or a task mismatch.

## 8. Task Types

| Task Type | What it tests | Example scenario |
|---|---|---|
| **Functional message** | All four dimensions at A1–B1, real everyday communication | Write a short email to a landlord about a leak in your flat. |
| **Informational writing** | Task Achievement, Content Quality, Coherence at B1–B2 | Write a short article for a community newsletter about a local change. |
| **Argumentative response** | Coherence and Language Control at B2–C1 | Respond to an opinion piece, taking a position and supporting it with reasoning. |
| **Extended analytical writing** | Full dimension test at C1–C2 | Write a structured analysis or recommendation on a given scenario, for a specified professional or academic reader. |

Task difficulty is calibrated to CEFR level by cognitive and linguistic demand, not topic complexity.

## 9. Validity & Reliability

**Why these tasks measure the construct**
CEFR is explicitly a functional, action-oriented framework — it is defined by what a learner can *do* with the language. Tasks that require candidates to complete authentic communicative acts map directly onto CEFR Can-Do descriptors, which are the framework's own definition of each level. This is a tighter validity argument than receptive-skills tests that approach CEFR indirectly.

**How consistency is ensured**
- Tasks are calibrated to CEFR descriptors by design
- Dimension rubrics are mapped to level features from the CEFR Companion Volume (2020)
- Self-contained task design removes topic-knowledge noise
- Binary scoring reduces rater drift compared to fine-grained numeric scales

**Main threats to validity and mitigations**
- **Target-level mismatch.** A candidate may attempt a level below or above their actual ability. Mitigation: adaptive task selection, or multiple tasks spanning levels.
- **Spoken-modality gap.** Written-only assessments don't capture full CEFR profile. Mitigation: parallel spoken FEAT CEFR planned as separate modality.
- **AI-assisted responses.** AI can produce plausible responses across CEFR levels. Mitigation: proctoring, task design with personal or scenario-specific hooks that generic AI handles less well.
- **Cultural communication conventions.** What counts as coherent, appropriate communication varies. Mitigation: rubric focused on functional success with the specified reader, not conformity to one style.

## 10. Framework Alignment *(not optional for this product)*

Unlike other FEAT products, framework alignment is core to FEAT CEFR — the construct *is* the framework.

- **Common European Framework of Reference for Languages (CEFR)** — 2001 and 2020 Companion Volume. The construct definition itself.
- **Global Scale of English (GSE)** — optional secondary calibration for Pearson-aligned contexts.

FEAT CEFR does **not** align with specific test-vendor band scales (Cambridge main suite scores, IELTS bands, TOEFL iBT scores). Those are separate calibrations used by their respective tests; FEAT CEFR aligns directly with the framework, not through a vendor intermediary. This is a positioning choice — conversion tables can be provided, but they are not the construct.

## 11. Use Cases

**Where it is used**
- Language proficiency certification for work, study, and immigration
- Employer screening of candidate language ability
- Pre-entry language checks for academic pathways (paired with FEAT Academic where academic reasoning is also needed)
- Language programme exit assessment
- Workforce language credentialling

**Decisions that can be made from results**
- Meets CEFR level X for role, programme, or visa requirements
- Ready / not yet ready for environment requiring CEFR level X
- Level certification awarded / not awarded

**Decisions that should NOT be made from results**
- Academic reasoning readiness — use FEAT Academic alongside
- Workplace judgement — use FEAT Professional alongside
- Specific job-role fit beyond language
- Domain expertise in any field
- Spoken language ability (until spoken modality launches)
