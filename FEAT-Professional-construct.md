## 1. Purpose

FEAT Professional measures how well someone performs in realistic workplace scenarios. It is designed for employers, training providers, and certification bodies that need evidence of applied capability — not knowledge of business theory.

Target users include:
- Job candidates being screened for roles
- Existing employees being assessed for development or promotion
- Participants in workforce programmes where outcomes must be demonstrated
- Individuals seeking a credential that shows they can do the work

Outcomes support screening decisions, development planning, and certification.

---

## 2. Core Construct

**This assessment measures the ability to perform effectively in workplace scenarios, demonstrating judgement, communication, and decision-making in context.**

**Successful performance looks like:** the candidate understands what the scenario demands, makes a reasoned decision or recommendation, communicates it clearly to the intended audience, and shows awareness of trade-offs and constraints.

**Failure (Not Yet Competent) looks like:** misreading the scenario, producing a generic answer that does not address the specific situation, failing to weigh trade-offs, or communicating in a way that would not land with the intended audience.

---

## 2a. Construct Boundaries

FEAT Professional deliberately does **not** measure:

- **Technical domain expertise** — engineering skill, legal knowledge, medical knowledge, and similar. Use a domain-specific test for that.
- **Past job performance** — FEAT Professional judges a response to a scenario, not a career history.
- **Personality or behavioural traits** — introversion, resilience, "grit", etc. are out of scope.
- **Cultural fit for a specific organisation** — construct is cross-organisational by design.
- **Spoken or presentation ability** — this is a written-response assessment.
- **English language proficiency as an ability in its own right** — use FEAT CEFR where language is the primary construct. Language control is only considered here where it obscures meaning.

These boundaries exist to prevent misuse: a candidate who scores Distinction on FEAT Professional has demonstrated workplace judgement and communication, not that they are the right hire, the right domain expert, or the right cultural fit.

---

## 3. Dimensions of Performance

| Dimension | What it represents | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Task Achievement** | Does the response actually address the scenario and do what was asked? | Without this, nothing else matters — a beautifully worded response to the wrong question fails. |
| **Content Quality** | Is the substance of the response accurate, relevant, and appropriate to context? | Workplace performance requires substantive answers, not just plausible-sounding ones. |
| **Practical Reasoning** | Does the candidate weigh trade-offs, surface constraints, and make defensible decisions? | Real work involves judgement under uncertainty — this is where senior candidates separate from junior ones. |
| **Communication Clarity** | Is the response pitched correctly for the audience, well-structured, and actionable? | Workplace communication succeeds or fails on whether the reader can use it. |

---

## 3a. The Product Twist

**FEAT Professional = workplace judgement, not workplace knowledge.** The test is whether you can actually handle the situation, not whether you can define the concept.

---

## 4. Performance Levels (FEAT Scale)

### Not Yet Competent
The response misses what the scenario actually requires. May produce a generic answer, show no evidence of weighing trade-offs, or communicate in a way that would not work in the intended context. A decision-maker reading this response would not act on it.

### Competent
The response addresses the scenario correctly, shows basic reasoning, and communicates clearly enough to be actioned. It may be safe rather than sophisticated, but it does the job. A decision-maker reading this response could reasonably act on it.

### Merit
The response goes beyond the basics: it identifies trade-offs, surfaces relevant constraints, and communicates with structure and clarity. Shows the judgement of someone who has actually done this kind of work.

### Distinction
The response is not just correct — it reframes or improves the problem. Identifies considerations the scenario didn't flag, anticipates follow-up questions, and communicates with the precision and pitch of a senior practitioner. The kind of answer that would make a hiring manager take notice.

## 5. Task Design Principles

**Every task must:**
- Present a realistic workplace scenario with enough context to act on
- Be self-contained — no reward for prior domain knowledge beyond what the scenario provides
- Require a decision, recommendation, or explanation (not just description)
- Include a defined audience (who is this response for?) so communication can be evaluated

**Every task must avoid:**
- Industry-specific jargon or trivia that privileges candidates from one sector
- Questions that test business-school concepts rather than applied judgement
- Scenarios that reward memorisation over reasoning
- Ambiguity that makes multiple valid answers impossible to score consistently

---

## 6. Evidence Model

The candidate's written response is the sole source of evidence. From that response we capture:

- Whether the response addresses the scenario as posed (Task Achievement signal)
- Whether the substantive points made are accurate and appropriate (Content Quality signal)
- Whether the response shows evidence of weighing options, surfacing constraints, or considering consequences (Practical Reasoning signal)
- Whether the response is structured, pitched to the audience, and actionable (Communication Clarity signal)

**We explicitly ignore:**
- Spelling and minor grammatical errors, unless they obscure meaning
- Length for its own sake
- Use of business buzzwords without substance
- Prior knowledge of specific industries, companies, or frameworks

**Minimum Evidence Requirement**

A response must contain enough substance to evaluate all four dimensions. Where it does not, the result is recorded as **Insufficient Evidence** rather than Not Yet Competent. This is a deliberate distinction — it protects fairness (a candidate who refuses or misreads a task is not the same as a candidate who engages and performs poorly) and data quality.

A response is marked Insufficient Evidence when:
- It is too short to evaluate (e.g. a single sentence, a refusal, or an off-topic response)
- It addresses a different task than the one posed
- It contains no substantive content that maps to any of the four dimensions

In this case, the assessment is flagged for review or re-attempt rather than scored.

---

## 7. Scoring Model

Analytic scoring on each of the four dimensions, producing a 4-tier judgement per dimension (Not Yet Competent → Competent → Merit → Distinction).

An overall performance level is derived from the dimension profile rather than a simple average:

- **Task Achievement acts as a gate.** A response that does not address the scenario cannot score above Not Yet Competent overall, regardless of other dimensions.
- **Merit or Distinction overall** requires Merit-or-higher in at least three of four dimensions.
- **The full dimension profile is preserved in reporting**, so a candidate strong on reasoning but weak on communication is identifiable to decision-makers.

**Handling conflicting dimension scores**

Divergent profiles (e.g. strong Content Quality, weak Task Achievement) are a real signal, not noise. FEAT Professional handles them as follows:

- The Task Achievement gate applies first. If the response does not address the scenario, overall is Not Yet Competent regardless of how strong other dimensions look.
- Where no gate applies but dimensions diverge significantly (e.g. Distinction on one, Not Yet Competent on another), the assessment is flagged for reviewer attention rather than averaged into a misleading middle score.
- The dimension profile is always shown to decision-makers, so they can see where the strength and weakness actually sit.

## 8. Task Types

| Task Type | What it tests | Example scenario |
|---|---|---|
| **Workplace scenario** | All four dimensions, with emphasis on Task Achievement and Content Quality | You've inherited a team project that's behind schedule. Write a short update for your manager explaining where things stand and what you propose to do. |
| **Decision-making** | Emphasis on Practical Reasoning | Your team has three possible vendors for a new tool. You have limited information on each. Recommend one and explain your reasoning. |
| **Advising / Explaining** | Emphasis on Communication Clarity and Content Quality | A colleague new to the company asks how to approach [situation]. Write a reply that would help them act. |

## 9. Validity & Reliability

**Why these tasks measure the construct**
Workplace performance is fundamentally about handling real situations. FEAT Professional tasks are designed to simulate those situations directly — requiring decisions, recommendations, or explanations in context, with a defined audience. This surfaces the same capabilities a line manager would evaluate in the flow of work.

**How consistency is ensured**
- A shared dimension rubric is applied to every response, reducing rater drift.
- Task Achievement acts as a scoring gate, preventing inflated scores for eloquent but off-target responses.
- Self-contained task design removes variance caused by prior-knowledge advantages.

**Main threats to validity and mitigations**
- **Context mismatch.** A scenario written for one work culture may not land the same way for a candidate in another. Mitigation: a scenario bank that spans contexts, or client-customised variants.
- **Writing ability as confound.** Weak written communication may mask strong reasoning. Mitigation: the four dimensions are scored separately, so reasoning strengths surface even when communication is weaker.
- **AI-assisted responses.** Candidates may use external AI to generate responses. Mitigation: proctoring where appropriate, or task design that rewards judgement off-the-shelf AI handles poorly.

## 11. Use Cases

**Where it is used**
- Recruitment and candidate screening
- Workforce development and promotion decisions
- Workforce training programme outcomes measurement
- Individual credentialling for professionals seeking evidence of applied capability

**Decisions that can be made from results**
- Shortlist / do-not-shortlist (screening)
- Ready for role / needs development (promotion)
- Programme passed / not passed (training)
- Credential awarded / not awarded (certification)

**Decisions that should NOT be made from results**
- Firing decisions — FEAT Professional is not an evaluation of past job performance
- Specific technical competency — use a domain-specific test for that
- Fit for a particular company culture — construct does not measure culture fit
